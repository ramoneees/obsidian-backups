# How Can I Be a Godly Father?

*Joel Smit walks through Old Testament fatherhood examples — failures and lessons for today.*

## The Furniture Manual Analogy

The article opens with a recently purchased piece of furniture that came with an instruction booklet. The "what-not-to-do" pictures came from common pitfalls that led Allen-wrench craftsmen astray from properly constructing a fine piece of particleboard furniture. Yet the "what-not-to-dos" proved especially helpful — they saved the writer from much hand-wringing frustration.

**Similarly**, through His Word, the Lord provides instruction for fathers that can save us from soul-wrenching pitfalls — pitfalls that can lead to our downfall and the downfall of our children.

## The Biblical Pattern

The Scriptures are filled with divine instructions of what to do as fathers — and what not to do — as the Apostle Paul's dual instruction shows:

> "Fathers, do not provoke your children to anger, but bring them up in the discipline and instruction of the Lord." — **Ephesians 6:4**

Along with instruction, the Scriptures provide plenty of positive and negative examples of fatherhood. **The negative examples seem to be particularly poignant**, especially when we read of many godly men whose children did not follow in their father's way or had major failings.

A prevalent and pernicious sin in a child — perhaps if it had been addressed by the father — might not have ended in the way that it did.

## The Author's Framework

The Old Testament stories are given by God for more than just parental instruction, but at the same time, they are **not less than that**. They offer us both warnings and opportunities to learn how to best lead our children (and ourselves) toward godliness.

**No, we cannot be our children's savior.** But by God's grace, we as their fathers can proactively point them to Jesus Christ, who not only saves us from sins committed, but also from greater sins and consequences that might be prevented.

## Old Testament Case Studies (Genesis)

### Adam: The Anger of Cain

Adam and Eve's sin in Genesis 3 shows its effect in the first family almost immediately. Cain and Abel are barely introduced before a domestic conflict:

- Cain angry at God's acceptance of his brother over him.
- God confronts Cain directly (Gen. 4:6) — not enough to turn him from his anger.
- Murder of Abel → Cain destined to be a fugitive and wanderer.

**Questions raised**: Were there signs of such temper and anger in Cain that could have been addressed? Did Adam intercede — or did he sit idly by as he had done when Eve was deceived by the serpent?

## Application

- **Don't rely on personal godliness to transmit to your children.** Active, intentional discipleship is required.
- **Address warning signs early.** Cain's anger was visible before it became murder.
- **Read Genesis fatherhood stories as pastoral case studies**, not just theology.
- **Pray for the proactive work of pointing children to Christ** — only He saves, but He uses faithful fathers.
