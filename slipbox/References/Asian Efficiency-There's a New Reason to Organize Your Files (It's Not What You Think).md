# There's a New Reason to Organize Your Files (It's Not What You Think)

*The old "find your files later" argument was always true. There's a newer reason most people haven't thought through: **your AI tools need it just as much as you do.***

## What Changed

The author has been building AI agents for the past year — tools that retrieve documents, search transcripts, pull context from meeting notes, surface relevant info automatically.

The agents that work well have one thing in common: **the underlying files are organized.**

The agents that keep failing? Often the same root cause:

- Poorly named files.
- Folders with nothing but dates.
- Documents called "final v3 REVISED USE THIS."
- Notes that have content but no searchable label.

**AI does not browse folders the way you do. It searches.** If your files are named in a way that only made sense to past-you in that specific moment, the agent either cannot find what it needs or grabs the wrong thing and confidently runs with it. A lot of "AI failures" are actually organization failures.

## The Plaud Experiment (Workflow Interviews)

A full day on-site at a health clinic with a Plaud recording pin running the whole time. Came home with a lot of audio files.

- **Files labeled clearly** (date + department + workflow) were easy to process — an AI agent could search for the right session, pull the transcript, work with it cleanly.
- **Files labeled generically** were harder to work with — even knowing what was in them, finding the right clip took longer than it should.

Scale that to months of files and dozens of documents and the difference becomes significant.

## The Three-Keyword Rule

Naming system used by the author, takes ~10 extra seconds per file:

> Think of three keywords that **future-you** would search to find this file. That is the name.

Format:

1. **Date first** — `YYYY-MM-DD` so files sort chronologically.
2. **Then the keywords.**
3. **Tag prefixes** for category: `ae` for Asian Efficiency, `tps` for podcast episodes, then client/tool/topic keywords.

Example: instead of `Meeting notes Dec 17`, the file becomes `2025-12-17 tps file organization ai automation`.

Future-you can find it by searching any of those words. Any AI agent pointed at the files can find it too. The name is self-describing enough that even without opening the file you know what is in it.

(Underscores vs spaces: underscores are a DOS-era legacy — spaces are fine. Just pick one and be consistent.)

## Why This Matters More Than It Used To

A concept the author thinks about a lot:

> Your files and transcripts are the raw material that makes AI work for you. Not the AI tools — those are cheap and getting cheaper. **The real asset is the organized, searchable library of your own work.**

Concrete payoff examples:

- Well-named meeting transcripts → input for a weekly synthesis agent that surfaces patterns across months of conversations.
- Well-named project files → AI assistant pulls relevant context before a client call without you having to remember which document has what.
- Well-organized notes → foundation for a knowledge base an agent can actually use.

**None of that works if the underlying files are a mess.** Every organized file is a small investment in the infrastructure that makes the AI stack actually functional.

## The Practical Test (Before Saving Any File)

Two questions:

1. Would future-me be able to find this by searching?
2. Would an AI agent searching for this content actually retrieve the right file?

If no to either → rename before saving.
