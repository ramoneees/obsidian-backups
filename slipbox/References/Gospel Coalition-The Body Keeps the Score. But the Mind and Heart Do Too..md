# The Body Keeps the Score. But the Mind and Heart Do Too.

*A TGC engagement with Bessel van der Kolk's framework and a recent critique of it.*

## Background: The Book's Influence

Since 2014, **The Body Keeps the Score** by Bessel van der Kolk has been one of the most influential and widely read books on trauma. 3M+ copies sold. Eight years on the NYT bestseller list. Recommended by mental health professionals, trauma survivors, and many others who found the content helped make sense of their experiences and relationships.

Van der Kolk's core claim: **trauma significantly affects the body, reshaping the brain by imprinting on the nervous system and causing physiological responses that persist even when the person is no longer experiencing or consciously thinking about the trauma.**

## What Van der Kolk Got Right

He was reacting to a real failure to understand trauma — culturally and within the church. The previous pattern (minimizing trauma or suggesting it can be resolved through purely spiritual means) was deeply painful and dismissive.

- Trauma isn't primarily a spiritual problem. It's the **whole-person response to real harm.**
- We are embodied souls. Our bodies do matter. Good care engages the whole person.
- Helping someone find calm in her body and relief from overwhelming stress honors the body God gave her.

## What the Recent Critique Says

A new study in **Frontiers in Systems Neuroscience** addresses potential reductionism in van der Kolk's framework. Not the first critique (others have raised concerns about misinterpretation of research or conclusions drawn more strongly than evidence supports), but this one goes further: it overturns van der Kolk by placing **active cognitive and interpretive processes** at the center of the trauma response.

### The Computer-Model Analogy

- Brain's processing of trauma → **erroneous predictions** about present and future events → triggers body's stress response even when no real danger exists.
- Heightened bodily reactions and hypervigilance = result of **subconscious predictions about danger shaped by past traumatic experiences**.
- Trauma contributes to cognitive rigidity and an ongoing assumption of threat.
- **The body is reacting to the mind's assessment, not in spite of it.**

In short: active mental processes are integral to the trauma response — affirming greater human complexity than van der Kolk's reductionistic model.

## Why This Matters for Treatment

Recognizing that bodily stress responses exist **in relationship with mental processes** explains why varied interventions can be effective:

- Contrary to van der Kolk's conclusions, substantial evidence supports **both mind-based and body-based interventions**.
- Makes sense if they're understood as interconnected and mutually influential.

## Where Christian Theology Adds

Secular research rarely addresses the role of the spiritual in human struggle. As Christian believers, we can:

- Appreciate the Frontiers article's highlighting of the **interconnectedness of mind and body**.
- Add the **spiritual dimension** — a third layer that the secular model can't fully account for.
