# Why Your Organization System Should Get Simpler as Your Work Gets More Complex

**Source:** [Asian Efficiency](https://www.asianefficiency.com/organization/simpler-organization-as-complexity-grows/)
**Author:** Thanh Pham
**Category:** Organization
**Published:** 16 June 2026

## Summary

Counterintuitive argument: as workload grows, most people add structure (new folders, more apps, more labels). But complexity compounds — every new layer adds decision overhead on the way in and search overhead on the way out. Eventually you're managing the system, not the work.

## Core Principle

> **As your world gets more complex, your organization should get simpler.**

### The Rule

> "Every type of thing has one home. Not two, not 'it depends' — one."

When something has five possible homes, it effectively has none. The constraint of one forces clarity: you decide once where this type of thing lives, then never think about it again.

### Thanh's Personal Setup

- 80% of working files → Google Drive
- Active docs, notes, transcripts → one place, named consistently, searchable
- Tasks → dedicated task manager (Todoist)
- Dropbox → archival only

> "Not because Google Drive is perfect. Because one searchable location beats five perfectly organized locations."

## The New Reason (Post-AI Era)

The original argument for consolidated organization was for *yourself* — fewer places to look, less mental overhead. **Now there's a second reason that most people haven't considered yet: your AI tools need to find your files too.**

When you build agents (meeting prep, weekly synthesizers, content systems), they need to retrieve files from your storage. Scattered files with inconsistent naming = agents working with incomplete information.

> "Organization used to be something you did for yourself so you could find things. Now it's infrastructure for every tool you put on top of it. The better your foundation, the better everything running on it performs."

## Action Steps

1. **Consolidate, don't reorganize** — first move is consolidation, not renaming
2. Pick one place for active working documents; move everything in that category there
3. Apply the "one home" rule to all file types
4. Use a consistent naming convention (searchable, not hierarchical)
5. Audit periodically: kill tools/apps you no longer need (or never needed)

## Key Takeaway

> "More structure creates more friction. And friction is what kills productivity."
