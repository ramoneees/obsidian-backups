# What a Doctor Taught Me About Which Work AI Should Actually Do

*A workflow interview with a concierge-medicine physician reframes how to think about AI and knowledge work.*

## The Physician's Setup

Boutique concierge medicine practice, sees premium patients, manages a small clinical team. Three concrete AI uses already in production:

1. **Patient counseling summaries** — Gemini/ChatGPT to write the lifestyle/dietary narrative in five sentences; she reviews, adjusts, shares with the patient. Was 20 min/patient before.
2. **AI scribe built into the EMR** — transcribes consultations in the background while she talks with patients. She used to chart during appointments (typing while listening and responding — bad for everyone).
3. **Lab-trend summarization** — clinical assistant in her EMR pulls up changes in liver enzymes or blood panels across a patient's history instead of manually scanning hundreds of reports.

**None of this involves diagnosis. All of it is paperwork.**

## The Line That Changed the Author's Thinking

> "My time should be used for diagnosing. Not charting."

The article's reframing: **AI creates the most value in professional work not by replacing expert judgment — the part that requires training, experience, and accountability — but by eliminating the overhead that surrounds it.**

## The Expertise vs Overhead Split (Universal Pattern)

Every knowledge worker has two buckets:

- **Expertise work**: actually requires you (your training, license, judgment, accountability).
- **Overhead**: writing things up, organizing info, following up, summarizing, explaining, preparing.

| Profession | Expertise | Overhead |
|------------|-----------|----------|
| Doctor | Diagnosis | Charting, write-ups, record review |
| Lawyer | Legal judgment | Research, drafting, summarization |
| Executive | Strategic decisions, key relationships | Calendar, email triage, meeting prep |

**AI is best at the overhead.** The author has seen this across many engagements: an EA went from 20–25 hrs/week on admin to 4–5 hrs/week after AI agents handled routine work. She didn't lose her job — she got her time back for work that actually required her.

## The Question Most People Ask About AI (And Why It's the Wrong One)

> "Will AI replace me?" → usually points to the expertise work. Will AI diagnose patients? Argue cases? Make strategic decisions? In most fields, for now, no.

**But that's not where the time is going anyway. The time goes to the overhead.**

## A Useful Test for Your Own Work

Before your next task, ask: **does this require my expertise, or just my time?**

- Writing up meeting notes after a call → time, not expertise. AI can do it.
- Deciding what the next move should be based on those notes → expertise.
- Drafting a patient counseling summary → time, not expertise. (The doctor already does this.)
- Reviewing the diagnosis plan for a complex case → expertise.
