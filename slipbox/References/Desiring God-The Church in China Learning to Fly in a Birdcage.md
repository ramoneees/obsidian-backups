# The Church in China: Learning to Fly in a Birdcage

*A Desiring God article on the state of Christianity under post-2018 religious restrictions — useful primer for prayer and contextual understanding.*

## The Birdcage Metaphor (Origin)

In 1980, a Chinese Party leader described the emerging Chinese economy as a "birdcage." The bird (nascent free market) could be allowed to fly only within the confines of a state-planned economy. **Chinese capitalism must be controlled by the Communist Party.**

The metaphor describes China today — not just the economy but **all of society, including religious life**. The government claims citizens have "freedom of religious belief." What it means: people can believe what they want, but the government decides how those beliefs can be practiced. Religious beliefs must take a back seat to Party ideology.

## The Birdcage Cycle

- **Late 1990s–2000s**: cage expanded → urban house-church movement flourished, Christians engaged social needs, Christian content proliferated online.
- **After 2012**: cage began to shrink. New regulations restricted civil society and religious life — limits on cross size, stricter clergy qualifications, prohibitions on using the internet to conduct religious services. A new wave of persecution, particularly against more prominent house churches.

## Life Inside the Birdcage

**Registered churches** (overseen by Party-led religious-affairs bureau):

- Years of being told to eschew politics have given way to expectations that churches serve the Party-state through the **Sinicization** campaign — aligning work and teaching with "socialist values" as defined by the Party.
- Laws prohibiting religious teaching to anyone under 18 are now strictly enforced. **Children are not allowed to attend church.**

**Unregistered churches** (house churches, no legal status, perceived as threat):

- Overt persecution and pressure.
- "Red lines" — getting too big, political advocacy, ties with foreign groups, organizing online — may trigger shutdown and pastor arrest.
- Examples named in the article:
  - **Pastor Wang of Early Rain Church** — nearing the end of a 9-year sentence for "inciting subversion of state power."
  - **Pastor Jin of Zion Church** — currently held for "illegal use of information networks."

## Pressure Below the Headlines

The high-profile cases don't represent everyone. Many churches experience "pressure" rather than crackdowns:

- Pastors invited to "drink tea" (euphemism for police questioning).
- Landlords pressured not to rent to religious groups → forced to meet in different locations weekly.
- Local officials encourage congregations to move to other neighborhoods.

## The Adaptation Pattern

> "The birdcage may be shrinking, but the life of faith inside it is still real, active, and full of witness."

Contradictions and adaptations:

- Many churches remain small and quiet — not on the receiving end of harsh crackdowns.
- Some shut-down churches don't cease to exist — they **divide into smaller fellowships, thereby actually multiplying.**
- Geography matters: more open in larger cities and coastal provinces, tighter in inland regions.
