# Why Hosting One Dinner Will Do More for Your Network Than 100 Networking Events

**Source:** [Asian Efficiency](https://www.asianefficiency.com/social/why-hosting-one-dinner-will-do-more-for-your-network-than-100-networking-events/)
**Author:** Thanh Pham
**Category:** Social
**Published:** 16 June 2026

## Summary

When Thanh moved to Austin in 2014, he spent a year attending meetups, happy hours, and networking events with name tags and bad appetizers — and still didn't really know anyone. In 2017 he flipped the script: started hosting small Jeffersonian-style dinners (~8 people) every other week for two years. That single habit built a network of founders, investors, operators, advisors, and friends he still talks to weekly. Argument: **hosting > attending**, at any scale.

## The Setup

- **Frequency:** every other week for ~2 years (≈ 50 dinners)
- **Format:** 8 people around a table, Jeffersonian-style
- **Curation:** 1–2 people Thanh knew well + a few recently-met interesting people + 1–2 brand-new with good vibe
- **No agenda, no pitching, no structured "networking" activity** — just dinner and conversation
- **Scale:** dinners in his apartment, food he cooked or ordered, good table, decent wine

## What Actually Happens When You Host

1. **Guests start introducing you to others** — when someone asks "how do you know Thanh?" and the answer is "he hosted this incredible dinner," it creates a story people share.
2. **Connections form between guests** — Thanh recounts: a woman told him she met her co-founder at one of his dinners. The startup was already helping hundreds of people. He had no idea the dinner would lead to that.
3. **You become "the human router"** — the node everyone passes through.

## Why Hosting Beats Attending

> "When you attend, you're there to get something — contacts, opportunities, maybe a job lead. Even if you're not aware of it, that energy comes through. People can feel it. And it makes real connection harder."

When you host, the job is to make sure everyone else has a good time. You ask questions because you genuinely want to know who people are so you can connect them with the right people. You listen more than you talk.

**Practical piece:** as the host, you become the connector by default. You know everyone in the room. Every new person gets introduced through you.

## The Curation Secret

> "The most important ingredient was curation. I was thoughtful about who I invited and why. I'd think about which two people in the room would really hit it off and make sure they ended up next to each other."

That level of intention — even at small scale — is what made people want to come back and bring their friends.

## Key Takeaways

- **Don't scale up before you have the curation discipline.** Eight people, done well, beats 50 people done poorly.
- **The role of "host" is a permanent compounding advantage** — it accumulates connections, stories, and reputation over time.
- **"Networking" is mostly a false economy when you're a passive attendee.**
- Cooking/hosting yourself = authenticity signal. People remember dinners more than conferences.
